Lesson Plan: Animal Detectives – Adaptations & Habitats

Grade Level: Upper Elementary (4th–5th Grade)
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Kentucky Science Standards Alignment

  • Use observable physical structures and behaviors to explain how plants and animals survive in their environment.
  • Identify how adaptations help organisms find food, water, shelter, and avoid predators in local habitats.
  • Recognize that changes in habitats can affect survival of species.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and describe habitats (arboreal, aquatic, terrestrial) found in the Floyds Fork parklands.
  • Explain how animal structures (body parts) and behaviors (activities, feeding, movement) help wildlife survive in Floyds Fork.
  • Use evidence and scientific vocabulary to solve an animal mystery and explain how adaptations connect animals to their habitat.

Key Vocabulary

  • Habitat – The natural environment where an organism lives and gets food, water, shelter, and space.
  • Adaptation – A structure or behavior that helps an animal survive.
  • Structure – A physical characteristic of an animal that helps it survive (fur, claws, beak shape).
  • Behavior – An action or activity an animal performs to survive (nocturnal hunting, building nests).
  • Arboreal – Lives mostly in trees.
  • Aquatic – Lives mostly in water.
  • Terrestrial – Lives mostly on land.
  • Herbivore – Eats plants.
  • Omnivore – Eats plants and animals.
  • Carnivore – Eats other animals.
  • Diurnal – Active during the day.
  • Crepuscular – Active at dawn and dusk.
  • Nocturnal – Active at night.

Materials

  • 5–6 Mystery Bags (each representing an animal found in or similar to Floyds Fork wildlife)
  • Animal adaptation artifacts (fur, feathers, webbed feet images, claws, camouflage patterns)
  • Animal Detective worksheets
  • Habitat posters or table sheets (Forest, Wetlands, Stream)
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Markers
  • Optional: photos or props from Floyds Fork

Guided Discovery Questions for Rangers

  • “What structure do you notice on this animal that might help it survive in its habitat?”
  • “What behavior does this animal have that helps it find food or avoid predators?”
  • “If this animal lived somewhere else in Floyds Fork, would it survive? Why or why not?”
  • “Which adaptations tell us this animal is arboreal, aquatic, or terrestrial?”
  • “How do human changes to Floyds Fork habitats affect animals’ survival?”

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Welcome students and explain they will become Animal Detectives investigating animals that live in habitats like those found at Floyds Fork.
~3 Introduction to Floyds Fork I Do: Introduce the parklands Ask: “Who has visited Floyds Fork?” “What animals have you seen there?” Show photos or props and explain that Floyds Fork includes forests, streams, and wetlands.
~4 Review Prior Learning We Do: Connect prior knowledge Ask: “What do animals need to survive?” “Have you learned about adaptations before?” Connect responses to Floyds Fork wildlife.
~5 Vocabulary: Habitats & Adaptations I Do: Introduce new terms Explain habitat types and define structure and behavior. Ask: “Which Floyds Fork animals have structures or behaviors that help them survive?”
~5 Mystery Bag Introduction I Do: Model investigation Model examining one artifact. Ask: “Which structure or behavior helps this animal survive?” Demonstrate worksheet expectations.
~25 Animal Detective Investigation You Do: Group inquiry Students investigate mystery bags and complete worksheets. Ranger circulates asking guided questions to connect adaptations to habitat and survival in Floyds Fork.
~8 Detective Reports We Do: Share findings Groups present conclusions. Ask: “Where in Floyds Fork might this animal live?” “Which structures and behaviors helped you decide?”
~3 Adaptation Reflection You Do: Student reasoning Ask: “Which structure or behavior is most important for survival?” “Could this animal survive in a different Floyds Fork habitat?”
~5 Wrap-Up & Questions We Do: Review & reflect Reinforce that protecting Floyds Fork habitats helps protect wildlife. Answer questions and congratulate students as Animal Detectives.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively share ideas and observations.
  • Vocabulary Understanding: Correctly use structure and behavior to explain adaptations.
  • Evidence-Based Reasoning: Students explain how artifacts show adaptations and survival strategies.
  • Connections to Floyds Fork: Students consistently tie animals and habitats back to the parklands.

Observation Tips

  • Listen for repeated references to Floyds Fork habitats, animal structures, and behaviors.
  • Check that students justify conclusions using structures and behaviors.
  • Ensure clear I Do / We Do / You Do structure throughout the lesson.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference during facilitation and observation.
