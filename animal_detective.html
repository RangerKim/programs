Lesson Plan: Animal Detectives – Adaptations & Habitats of Floyds Fork

Grade Level: 4th Grade
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork Parklands Connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and classify habitats (arboreal, aquatic, terrestrial) found in the Floyds Fork parklands and match animals to the habitats where they live.
  • Explain animal adaptations (body parts and behaviors) that help animals survive in Floyds Fork, including diet, activity patterns, and defense strategies.
  • Use evidence and scientific vocabulary to solve an animal mystery and explain how adaptations connect animals to their habitat in Floyds Fork.

Key Vocabulary

  • Habitat – The natural place where an animal lives and gets food, water, shelter, and space.
  • Adaptation – A body part or behavior that helps an animal survive.
  • Arboreal – Lives mostly in trees.
  • Aquatic – Lives mostly in water.
  • Terrestrial – Lives mostly on land.
  • Herbivore – Eats plants.
  • Omnivore – Eats plants and animals.
  • Carnivore – Eats other animals.
  • Diurnal – Active during the day.
  • Crepuscular – Active at dawn and dusk.
  • Nocturnal – Active at night.
  • Musk – A strong smell used for defense or communication.

Materials

  • 5–6 Mystery Bags (each representing an animal found in or similar to Floyds Fork)
  • Animal adaptation artifacts (fur, feathers, webbed feet images, claws, scent/musk sample, camouflage)
  • Animal Detective Worksheets
  • Habitat posters or table sheets (Forest, Wetlands, Stream)
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Markers
  • Optional: photos or props from Floyds Fork

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes (Floyds Fork Connections)
~5 Welcome & Hook: Become an Animal Detective I Do: Set purpose Introduce yourself as Floyds Fork rangers. Explain students will investigate animals that live in or depend on habitats like those found in Floyds Fork.
~10 Vocabulary & Habitat Warm-Up We Do: Build shared understanding Introduce arboreal, aquatic, terrestrial using Floyds Fork examples (trees, streams, forest floor). Act out vocabulary and connect each to a parkland habitat.
~5 Mystery Bag Instructions & Modeling I Do: Model investigation Demonstrate how an artifact (fur, webbed feet) shows an adaptation. Ask: “Why would this help an animal survive at Floyds Fork?”
~25 Animal Detective Group Investigation You Do: Small-group inquiry Students examine mystery bags and complete worksheets identifying habitat, adaptations, diet, activity time, and defense. Ranger circulates asking: “Where in Floyds Fork might this animal live?”
~10 Detective Reports & Class Discussion We Do: Share findings Groups present conclusions using vocabulary. Class guesses animal and discusses whether it could live in Floyds Fork and why.
~5 Wrap-Up & Reflection We Do: Connect & reflect Ask: “How do adaptations help animals survive in Floyds Fork?” Reinforce that protecting habitats protects animals.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Vocabulary Use: Students correctly use habitat and adaptation terms.
  • Evidence-Based Reasoning: Students explain how artifacts show adaptations.
  • Floyds Fork Connections: Students consistently relate animals and habitats back to the parklands.
  • Collaboration: Students work together to solve the animal mystery.

Observation Tips

  • Listen for repeated references to Floyds Fork habitats.
  • Ensure students justify conclusions using adaptations.
  • Check that I Do / We Do / You Do structure is clearly followed.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) supports ranger facilitation and observation.
