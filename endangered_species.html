Lesson Plan: Extinction, Endangered Species, and Habitats

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Learning Objectives

  • Define extinction, local extinction, and endangered species.
  • Recognize that both plants and animals can go extinct.
  • Identify major habitats and their defining characteristics.
  • Explain natural and human-caused reasons for extinction.
  • Describe examples of locally endangered species in Floyds Fork.
  • Suggest ways humans can help wildlife through conservation.

Key Vocabulary

  • Extinction – The complete disappearance of a species when no members remain alive anywhere on Earth.
  • Local extinction – When a species disappears from a specific area but still exists in other parts of the world.
  • Endangered species – A species that is at serious risk of becoming extinct due to low population numbers or threats to its survival.
  • Habitat – The natural environment where an organism lives and gets the food, water, shelter, and space it needs to survive.
  • Conservation – The protection and careful management of natural resources and wildlife to prevent damage or extinction.

Materials

  • Habitat posters or table sheets
  • Animal and plant cards
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Markers
  • Optional: photos or props from Floyds Fork

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Welcome students warmly, take attendance, give a brief preview of today’s lesson. Make students feel comfortable and excited about learning.
~3 Introduction to Floyds Fork I Do: Introduce the parklands Ask students: “Who has visited Floyds Fork?” Show photos or props. Explain our role as rangers and what we are doing at the school today.
~4 Review Prior Learning We Do: Connect prior knowledge Ask students what they have been learning in science. Prompt for vocabulary or concepts they recall (e.g., adaptations, extinction). Discuss responses and relate to Floyds Fork species and habitats.
~3 Vocabulary: Endangered Species I Do: Introduce new terms Define “endangered species.” Give examples from Floyds Fork. Ask students to restate definition in their own words.
~2 Broadening Extinction We Do: Discuss plants and animals Ask: “Can plants go extinct too?” Discuss with students and give local examples like native plants in Floyds Fork.
~3 Student Questions You Do: Student-led inquiry Allow students to ask questions. Be ready to explain briefly or prompt deeper thinking: “Could this happen in Floyds Fork?”
~2 Extinction Vocabulary I Do: Clarify terms Explain difference between extinct and locally extinct species. Provide local examples (e.g., bison, mountain lion).
~4 Habitat Brainstorm We Do: Identify habitats Ask students: “Which habitats exist in Floyds Fork?” Record answers. Prompt them to explain why each is a habitat (forest = trees, wetlands = water, etc.).
~3 Habitat Activity Introduction I Do: Model activity Show students how to read animal/plant cards and determine their habitat. Demonstrate one example. Check understanding.
~8 Habitat Activity You Do: Students place cards Students work independently or in small groups to place cards in habitats. Ranger circulates and asks: “Could we find this in Floyds Fork?” Prompt for reasoning from the cards.
~4 Habitat Completion We Do: Review results Discuss placement, highlight patterns, and connect to local ecosystems. Emphasize that this is a simplified representation.
~5 Habitat Walkthrough We Do: Explore habitats Walk around tables. Ask: “Do adaptations tell us why species are or aren’t in Floyds Fork?” Encourage discussion.
~4 Distribution & Survival We Do: Compare animals across habitats Ask students: “Why might this animal live in one or multiple habitats?” Discuss adaptations and environmental needs.
~3 Extinction Reflection You Do: Student reasoning Ask: “Was extinction caused by humans, nature, or both?” Record responses. Connect to local examples.
~4 Causes of Extinction We Do: Analyze causes Discuss natural vs. human causes. Examples: overhunting, pollution, habitat loss. Relate to Floyds Fork.
~3 Local Extinction I Do: Provide examples Explain local extinctions historically, e.g., mountain lions, bison. Discuss why they disappeared and conservation implications.
~5 Endangered Species Case Studies You Do: Student investigation Ask: “How are humans affecting these species locally?” Students identify causes (e.g., pollution, roads, deforestation). Ranger guides discussion.
~4 Conservation Solutions We Do: Brainstorm actions Discuss how humans can protect species. Introduce permits, hunting/fishing licenses, habitat restoration, and local park strategies.
~3 Wrap-Up & Questions We Do: Review & reflect Ask students what they learned. Answer final questions. Summarize key points and reinforce Floyds Fork examples.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively share ideas about Floyds Fork and habitats.
  • Vocabulary Understanding: Correctly define extinction, local extinction, endangered species.
  • Habitat Activity: Correct placement of plants/animals; can explain reasoning.
  • Connections to Floyds Fork: Students identify local examples of habitats, species, human impacts, and conservation measures.
  • Questioning & Thinking: Students answer prompts demonstrating reasoning about adaptations and survival.

Observation Tips

  • Check that each activity clearly shows I Do / We Do / You Do roles.
  • Look for students connecting concepts to Floyds Fork habitats and species.
  • Observe student engagement, reasoning, and ability to discuss adaptations and extinction causes.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference for the ranger during facilitation and observation. Labels in text clearly indicate the type of activity.
