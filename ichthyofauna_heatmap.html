Ichthyofauna of Floyds Fork – Heat Map Activity

Grade Band

  • 5–8

Duration

  • 60 minutes

Learning Setting

  • Hybrid (Indoor & Outdoor)

Phenomenon / Topic

  • Local fish species and their distribution along Floyds Fork creek and parklands

NGSS Alignment

  • MS-LS2-1: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the effects of resource availability on organisms and populations of organisms in an ecosystem.
  • MS-LS2-2: Construct an explanation that predicts patterns of interactions among organisms across multiple ecosystems.
  • Crosscutting Concepts: Systems and System Models, Patterns, Cause and Effect, Stability and Change

Learning Objectives

  • Identify local fish species and their preferred habitats along the creek and parklands.
  • Explain how environmental factors influence fish distribution.
  • Model and visualize fish population patterns with a heat map of the parklands.
  • Reflect on human and environmental impacts on local ichthyofauna.
  • Synthesize observations into a spatial analysis that communicates ecological insights.

Key Vocabulary

  • Ichthyofauna – fish species of a particular region
  • Habitat – the natural environment where an organism lives
  • Heat Map – a visual representation of data intensity or density
  • Ecosystem – a community of living organisms interacting with their environment
  • Interdependence – how organisms rely on each other and environmental factors

Materials

  • Large paper map or hand-drawn poster of Floyds Fork creek and parklands
  • Colored pencils, markers, or crayons
  • Field guides / printed fish ID sheets
  • Clipboards, notebooks, pencils
  • Yarn or string (optional for habitat zones)
  • Sticky notes or small labels for fish species
  • Magnifying glasses, nets, or simple water testing kits (optional)

Lesson Procedure

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
5 min Hook & Engagement I Do Ask: “Where do different fish species live along Floyds Fork creek and parklands, and why?” Show printed fish images. Leading Questions:
  • What might a fish need to survive in this area?
  • Why do some fish prefer pools while others prefer shallow areas?
  • How could plants or rocks affect where fish are found?
Indoor option: use classroom map or floor diagram of park.
5 min Explain Heat Map Task I Do Introduce goal: create a heat map showing fish distribution and habitats. Review legend and map layout. Leading Questions:
  • How can we show areas with lots of fish versus areas with few?
  • Which features of the creek or park might influence fish abundance?
  • What symbols or colors should we use to make our map easy to read?
Answer clarifying questions.
5 min Assign Observation Groups & Zones We Do Divide students into groups, assign sections of the creek/parklands. Leading Questions:
  • Which part of the park might have different types of habitats?
  • How can your group collect observations efficiently in your section?
Indoor option: assign sections on printed map or classroom floor plan.
15 min Outdoor Exploration / Data Collection We Do Students record water depth, flow, vegetation, and fish signs. Encourage discussion of patterns. Leading Questions:
  • Where do you see signs of fish activity? Why might fish be here?
  • How does water depth or flow affect which species are present?
  • What patterns do you notice along your section of the creek?
Indoor option: use photos, videos, or pre-recorded data.
10 min Begin Heat Map Creation You Do Students start marking fish distribution on the map using the legend. Leading Questions:
  • Which areas have the highest fish abundance and why?
  • Do different species appear in the same area or separate areas?
  • How can you show both species and habitat features clearly?
Encourage collaboration within groups.
10 min Finalize Heat Map & Annotate You Do Students add species names, habitat notes, and reasoning. Leading Questions:
  • Does your map match your observations? Why or why not?
  • What environmental features seem to influence fish distribution the most?
  • How could your map help others understand the creek ecosystem?
Check for consistency with observed data.
10 min Reflection & Share-Out You Do Groups share heat maps with class. Leading Questions:
  • Which habitats had the most fish? Why do you think that is?
  • Did any observations surprise you?
  • How could humans affect fish habitats here?
Indoor option: share maps digitally or on printed sheets.

Assessment / Evaluation

  • Formative: Participation in observation, discussion, and mapping activity
  • Summative: Completed heat map with annotations showing understanding of species-habitat relationships, cause-and-effect, and visual communication of patterns

Extensions / Adaptations

  • Math/Statistics: Count or estimate species abundance and create graphs or charts.
  • Language Arts: Write a narrative from a fish's perspective navigating the creek and parklands.
  • Art: Enhance heat maps with watercolors or illustrations.
  • Social Studies / Environmental Science: Discuss conservation strategies to protect creek habitats.
  • Adaptation: Indoor activities with images, videos, or printed maps if outdoor access is limited or weather is poor.

Educator Cheat Sheet

  • Emphasize observing patterns and cause-and-effect rather than exact species counts.
  • Ensure each student group has a clear section of the creek/park map for the heat map.
  • Encourage reasoning aloud during indoor or outdoor exploration.
  • Use sticky notes to adjust heat map predictions after discussion.
  • Indoor fallback: Provide pre-made habitat photos and data sheets for the same activity flow.

Deeper Concepts / Extension Notes

  • Discuss how environmental gradients (temperature, flow, vegetation) influence fish distribution.
  • Explore predator-prey relationships and resource competition in fish populations.
  • Introduce advanced mapping concepts like overlapping species zones or population density gradients.
  • Connect to local conservation efforts and watershed management.
  • Encourage students to think about long-term changes: climate change, urbanization, and human impact on creek ecosystems.
