Lesson Plan: Seasons Explorers – Floyds Fork Parklands

Grade Level: Elementary (adaptable for K–5)
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / optional outdoor visit to Floyds Fork parklands
Instructors: Rangers

Learning Objectives

  • Describe how plants change during the seasons using a physical, movement-based model.
  • Identify seasonal patterns (spring, summer, fall, winter) and relate them to plant cycles.
  • Explain how environmental factors (day length, temperature, sunlight) affect plant growth, bloom, dormancy, and “tired/stressed” states.
  • Connect observations of seasonal cycles to real-life examples in Floyds Fork, including actively farmed areas.
  • Optional higher-level: Connect seasonal changes to solstices and Kentucky growing season.

Key Vocabulary

  • Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter – The four seasons with distinct patterns of daylight and temperature.
  • Sprout – When a plant begins to grow from a seed, typically in spring.
  • Bud – Small unopened leaf or flower developing in late spring.
  • Grow – Stretching, developing, or putting energy into building leaves, stems, and roots. Repeated 10 times in the relay.
  • Bloom – Opening of flowers in summer. Controlled so plants do not touch each other.
  • Fruit – Ripening of seeds or produce, usually in late summer or fall.
  • Dormant – Resting phase in winter when plants conserve energy.
  • Tired / Stressed – When plants overextend growth, bloom too early, get too much sun, or have shortened dormancy. “Tired” is used for younger students; stressed is the scientific term.

Materials

  • Open space for relay
  • Cones or markers for start/finish
  • Batons (or small objects) for passing
  • Optional photos of Floyds Fork parklands in each season
  • Whiteboard/chart paper for discussion prompts

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Welcome students, introduce lesson focus on seasons and seasonal plant changes. Preview the relay activity and connect to Floyds Fork observations.
~4 Prior Knowledge & Discussion We Do: Ask: “What season is it now? How do plants or animals change?” “What did you notice at Floyds Fork in different seasons?” Record answers and tie to upcoming activity.
~5 Vocabulary Introduction I Do: Introduce season-related vocabulary (sprout, bud, grow, bloom, fruit, dormant, tired/stressed). Give examples from Floyds Fork or local farms.
~8 Plant Cycle Relay Game You Do: Students form teams and run a relay representing a plant’s seasonal cycle. Each step must be acted out physically before passing the baton. Movements include:
  1. Sprout (Spring) – Slowly rise like a shoot. Fingers wiggle like leaves. Students can invent gestures.
  2. Bud (Late Spring) – Form a fist or curl hands to show buds.
  3. Grow (Spring/Summer, Repeat 10 Times) – Stretch and bend 10 times to show energy-intensive growth.
  4. Bloom (Summer, Controlled) – Open arms, sway, or spin gently without touching others.
  5. Fruit (Late Summer/Fall) – Hug self or hold hands overhead for 10 seconds to show ripening.
  6. Dormant (Winter) – Crouch or freeze to represent resting phase until your team is completely quiet and back where they started to get ready to do the process again .

Variation Tie-In: Students naturally perform steps differently, just like real plants. Discuss:

  • Which “racers” grew faster or slower? Why?
  • How might early bloomers or fast growers compete for sunlight, water, or nutrients at Floyds Fork?
  • How does season length affect plant energy and “tired/stressed” states?
  • How can overexertion (too much growth, too long bloom, too much sun) cause stress?
Optional: Extra rounds to simulate shortened dormancy or extended growth. Students notice which plants get tired faster, connecting to seasonal changes and climate effects.
~5 Reflection & Discussion We Do: Ask guiding questions at each step:
  • “Which movements were easiest or hardest? Why?”
  • “How does this relate to what we see at Floyds Fork during each season?”
  • “What happens if winter is too short or summer too long?”
  • “How does variation among plants affect the ecosystem?”
~4 Optional Outdoor Observation (if time) You Do: Walk students around Floyds Fork to observe seasonal changes, buds, blooms, dormant plants, or active farmed fields. Ask: “Can you see the stages or energy use we acted out?”

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation in relay and discussion.
  • Ability to connect plant movements to seasonal changes.
  • Explanation of “tired/stressed” plants and causes.
  • Observational connections to Floyds Fork seasons and farmed areas.
  • Optional: Ability to discuss solstice timing and seasonal light cues for older students.

Observation Tips

  • Guide discussion with leading questions at each step.
  • Ensure grow is repeated 10 times, bloom is controlled.
  • Prompt students to notice variation among plant “racers.”
  • Connect reflections back to Floyds Fork’s seasonal cycles and climate effects.

Cheat Sheet for Rangers: “Tired” is an accessible way to describe plant stress from too much or too little growth, sunlight, or dormancy. Always tie activities to seasons: sprout → spring, bud → late spring, grow → spring/summer, bloom → summer, fruit → late summer/fall, dormant → winter. Variation among plants mirrors real plant diversity, competition, and resource use. Use Floyds Fork parklands, including still-farmed areas, for concrete examples.

Ranger Deep Dive / Optional Higher-Level

  • Plant Variation: Some racers grow or bloom faster/slower. Early bloomers may outcompete slower plants, showing competition for sunlight, water, and nutrients. Overexertion or shortened dormancy causes “tired” plants, impacting seasonal survival.
  • Seasonal Timing: Use the relay to show why timing matters: too early bloom, too long growth, or skipped dormancy stresses plants.
  • Solstices & Kentucky Growing Season:
    • Winter Solstice: shortest day → plants have less sunlight, reinforcing dormancy.
    • Summer Solstice: longest day → maximum sunlight for growth and bloom.
    • Kentucky growing season: typically mid-April to October. Students can observe active crops at Floyds Fork.
    • Discussion: How shorter winters or longer summers affect plant cycles, energy, and competition.
  • Park Connection: Always tie back to Floyds Fork parklands. Show how seasonal changes in sunlight, temperature, and farmed fields demonstrate the same cycles acted out in the relay.
  • Discussion Prompts: Which racers were fastest/slower? Did any get “tired”? How does this mirror plant stress or competition in real ecosystems?
