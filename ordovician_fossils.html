Lesson Plan: Ordovician Fossils at Floyds Fork

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Educators (___ & ___)

Learning Objectives

  • Identify fossils from the Ordovician period found in Floyds Fork.
  • Explain the process of fossilization, including the five main steps: death, burial, decay, mineralization, and discovery.
  • Understand preservation bias and why only hard parts often fossilize.
  • Use evidence and reasoning to reconstruct missing parts of fossils in illustrations.
  • Work collaboratively to create a scientific illustration that models an Ordovician sea creature in its environment.
  • Practice responsible fossil stewardship by following Leave No Trace principles.

Key Vocabulary

  • Fossil: The preserved remains, impression, or trace of a once-living organism from the past.
  • Fossilization: The process by which living material is preserved in the rock record.
  • Mineralization: Step in fossilization where minerals replace organic material in the hard parts.
  • Preservation Bias: The tendency for some organisms or parts (like shells or bones) to fossilize more easily than others.
  • Ordovician Period: A geologic time period ~485–443 million years ago, when most life was marine.
  • Scientific Illustration: A drawing based on observation and evidence, often used to depict organisms and habitats.
  • Leave No Trace: Enjoying natural areas responsibly by not removing or damaging plants, animals, rocks, or fossils so others can learn from them.

Materials

  • Fossils from Floyds Fork (or high-quality replicas/photos)
  • Whiteboard or chart paper
  • Markers, pencils, and crayons
  • Paper for group illustrations
  • Optional: reference images of Ordovician sea life

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Educator Notes
~3 Student Arrival & Introduction I Do: Set expectations Welcome students, introduce fossils and the Ordovician period. Ask: “Has anyone seen a fossil before?” Highlight that we must handle fossils carefully and follow Leave No Trace principles so everyone can learn from them.
~5 Prior Knowledge Check We Do: Discuss what students know Ask guiding questions:
  • What do you think fossils are?
  • How do you think something turns into a fossil?
  • Do you think all parts of an animal are equally likely to become a fossil?
  • Why is it important not to remove fossils from nature?
Record ideas on the board and note gaps or misconceptions. Emphasize Leave No Trace thinking.
~5 Introduce Fossilization Steps I Do: Explain key concepts Discuss the five steps of fossilization with guiding questions:
  • Death: “What might happen when this creature dies in the ocean?”
  • Burial: “How does sediment cover it?”
  • Decay: “Which parts decay first?”
  • Mineralization: “What do you think replaces the bones or shells over time?”
  • Discovery: “How do we find these fossils millions of years later?”
Use diagrams, mention that fossils are fragile and must be handled responsibly if brought in from Floyds Fork or observed on site.
~6 Observation & Evidence Discussion We Do: Analyze fossils Show fossils. Ask:
  • Which parts are present? Missing?
  • Why do you think only these parts are preserved?
  • What can we infer about the creature’s size, shape, or lifestyle?
  • How can we handle these fossils so they aren’t damaged for future learners?
Draw observations on the board to build a shared model. Demonstrate gentle handling and Leave No Trace principles.
~10 Scientific Illustrator Group Modeling We Do: Predict & model environment Ask students to describe what might have surrounded their fossil in the Ordovician sea:
  • What kind of water, plants, or other animals might have been nearby?
  • Where would it have rested or moved?
  • How can we model this without touching or taking fossils from nature?
Draw ideas on the board to make a shared model. Emphasize evidence-based reasoning and Leave No Trace thinking.
~15 Group Scientific Illustration You Do: Students draw Students work in small groups to create an illustration of their Ordovician creature and habitat. Remind them to:
  • Use the fossil as a base.
  • Fill in missing parts using evidence and imagination.
  • Consider adaptations for survival.
  • Handle any fossil materials carefully; do not remove from the park.
Circulate to guide reasoning and ensure Leave No Trace principles are followed.
~10 Group Presentations You Do: Share & explain Groups present illustrations. Ask:
  • What did you infer about missing parts?
  • How did you decide what environment or other organisms to include?
  • If higher level, discuss adaptations.
  • How did you ensure you followed Leave No Trace rules?
Encourage discussion and peer feedback.
~5 Reflection & Wrap-Up We Do: Summarize learning Ask students: “What surprised you about fossils? What did you have to infer?” Review vocabulary: fossilization, mineralization, preservation bias, adaptations. Emphasize Leave No Trace and caring for Floyds Fork resources.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively contribute to discussions, predictions, and drawings.
  • Evidence-Based Reasoning: Students support inferences about missing parts and habitats using fossil evidence.
  • Collaboration: Students work together to create coherent scientific illustrations.
  • Adaptation Thinking: Higher-level students explain possible adaptations for survival.
  • Fossil Stewardship: Students demonstrate understanding of Leave No Trace principles.
  • Presentation Skills: Students clearly describe their reconstructed creature and habitat.

Observation Tips

  • Check that students distinguish observed evidence vs. imaginative reconstruction.
  • Observe group discussion, reasoning, and use of vocabulary.
  • Look for engagement with fossils, the Ordovician environment, and Floyds Fork connections.
  • Ensure students are handling fossils carefully and applying Leave No Trace principles.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference for educators during facilitation and observation.

Educator Cheat Sheet

  • Fossil Examples: Brachiopods, trilobites, bryozoans, crinoid stems.
  • Fossilization Reminders: Only hard parts are usually preserved; soft tissues decay. Encourage prediction and imagination.
  • Guiding Questions: Ask students to justify why certain features are likely or unlikely.
  • Ordovician Environment: Shallow seas with diverse marine life. Prompt discussion of reefs, predators, and sediment.
  • Adaptation Tip: Ask: “How might this creature move, feed, or avoid predators?”
  • Leave No Trace & Fossil Care: Fossils are fragile and non-renewable. Handle gently or observe without touching. Never remove fossils from Floyds Fork so others can learn from them. Reinforce this throughout activities.

Deeper Educator Knowledge

  • Ordovician Geology: Floyds Fork sediments are primarily limestone and shale formed in a shallow sea environment. Understanding sediment deposition helps explain fossil preservation.
  • Fossilization Process: Beyond the five main steps, educators can explore permineralization, recrystallization, and molds/casts in detail.
  • Preservation Bias: Soft-bodied organisms rarely fossilize. Discuss why this affects our view of ancient ecosystems.
  • Ordovician Biodiversity: Study reef-building organisms, early cephalopods, and plankton to explain community structure.
  • Local Paleoecology: Consider predator-prey interactions, mobility, and feeding strategies of Ordovician marine life.
  • Scientific Illustration Tips: Techniques for reconstructing missing parts using symmetry, functional morphology, and comparisons with related organisms.
  • Leave No Trace Science Connection: Understanding the fragility and non-renewable nature of fossils strengthens the rationale for careful handling and conservation ethics.
