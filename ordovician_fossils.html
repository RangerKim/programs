Lesson Plan: Ordovician Fossils, Fossilization, & Scientific Illustration

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Educators: ____________________________

Learning Objectives

  • Explain what fossils are and why they are important evidence of Earth’s past.
  • Identify common Ordovician Period fossils found in the Floyds Fork region.
  • Describe the five-step process of fossilization, including mineralization.
  • Explain preservation bias and why soft parts of organisms are usually missing.
  • Use scientific evidence and imagination to reconstruct an ancient organism.

Key Vocabulary

  • Fossil – Preserved remains, impressions, or traces of ancient life.
  • Ordovician Period – A time about 485–444 million years ago when Kentucky was underwater.
  • Sediment – Small particles of sand, mud, or rock that settle in water.
  • Fossilization – The long process that turns living things into fossils.
  • Mineralization – When minerals carried by water replace or fill in the remains of an organism, turning it into stone.
  • Preservation Bias – The idea that some parts of organisms fossilize more easily than others.
  • Limestone – Sedimentary rock formed in ancient marine environments where fossils are often found.

Materials

  • Fossils or fossil replicas from Floyds Fork (brachiopods, bryozoans, crinoids, trilobites)
  • Drawing paper and pencils/colored pencils
  • Rock samples or limestone images
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Optional: map of ancient seas over Kentucky

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Educator Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Welcome Tell students they will explore ancient life from Floyds Fork and learn how fossils form.
~4 Floyds Fork Long Ago I Do: Set the scene Explain that Floyds Fork was once a shallow sea during the Ordovician Period.
~4 What Are Fossils? We Do: Build understanding Ask students what fossils are and clarify that fossils are usually hard parts, not whole animals.
~5 Ordovician Fossils I Do: Introduce examples Show local fossils and explain what kinds of organisms lived in the ancient sea.
~6 The 5 Steps of Fossilization I Do: Explicit instruction 1. An organism dies
2. It is quickly buried by sediment
3. Soft parts decay; hard parts remain
4. Mineralization replaces or fills remains with minerals
5. Sediment hardens into rock, forming a fossil
~4 Preservation Bias We Do: Concept connection Discuss why shells fossilize better than soft body parts.
~6 Scientific Illustrator Activity You Do: Group work Students work in groups with a fossil from Floyds Fork. They draw what the entire organism may have looked like when alive, adding missing soft parts using evidence from habitat and fossil clues.
~5 Prepare Presentations We Do: Organize thinking Groups plan how they will explain their drawing and reasoning.
~6 Group Presentations You Do: Share learning Each group presents their reconstructed sea creature, explaining:
  • What fossil they used
  • Which parts were missing due to preservation bias
  • Why they drew the added features
~4 Higher-Level Extension: Adaptations (Optional) We Do: Deepen thinking Ask advanced groups to explain how their organism’s features helped it survive (movement, feeding, protection).
~3 Wrap-Up & Review We Do: Summarize Review Ordovician life, fossilization steps, mineralization, and preservation bias.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Students correctly describe all five steps of fossilization.
  • Students explain preservation bias and missing soft parts.
  • Drawings show evidence-based reasoning tied to habitat.
  • Groups communicate ideas clearly during presentations.
  • Advanced students explain adaptations logically.

Note: Color coding I Do, We Do, and You Do

Educator Cheat Sheet

  • Key Fossils: Brachiopods, bryozoans, crinoids, trilobites
  • Big Idea: Fossils are incomplete clues due to preservation bias
  • Critical Question: “What does the fossil show—and what is missing?”
  • 5 Steps Shortcut: Die → Bury → Decay → Mineralize → Rock

Educator Deep Dive / Advanced Comprehension

  • Mineralization often involves calcite in limestone environments.
  • Ordovician seas supported diverse marine ecosystems.
  • Scientific illustrations are models based on evidence, not guesses.
  • Encourage multiple valid interpretations if reasoning is sound.
