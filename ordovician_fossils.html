Lesson Plan: Ordovician Fossils at Floyds Fork

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Educators (___ & ___)

Learning Objectives

  • Identify fossils from the Ordovician period found in Floyds Fork.
  • Explain the process of fossilization, including the five main steps: death, burial, decay, mineralization, and discovery.
  • Understand preservation bias and why only hard parts often fossilize.
  • Use evidence and reasoning to reconstruct missing parts of fossils in illustrations.
  • Work collaboratively to create a scientific illustration that models an Ordovician sea creature in its environment, connected to Floyds Fork.

Key Vocabulary

  • Fossil: The preserved remains, impression, or trace of a once-living organism from the past.
  • Fossilization: The process by which living material is preserved in the rock record.
  • Mineralization: Step in fossilization where minerals replace organic material in the hard parts.
  • Preservation Bias: The tendency for some organisms or parts (like shells or bones) to fossilize more easily than others.
  • Ordovician Period: A geologic time period ~485–443 million years ago, when most life was marine.
  • Scientific Illustration: A drawing based on observation and evidence, often used to depict organisms and habitats.

Materials

  • Fossils from Floyds Fork (or high-quality replicas/photos)
  • Whiteboard or chart paper
  • Markers, pencils, and crayons
  • Paper for group illustrations
  • Optional: reference images of Ordovician sea life

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Educator Notes
~3 Student Arrival & Introduction I Do: Set expectations Welcome students, introduce fossils and the Ordovician period. Ask: “Has anyone ever seen a fossil before?” Then connect to Floyds Fork: “Some of the fossils we study come from Floyds Fork. What clues about the land or streams might help preserve fossils?”
~5 Prior Knowledge Check We Do: Discuss what students know Ask guiding questions:
  • What do you think fossils are?
  • How do you think something turns into a fossil?
  • Do you think all parts of an animal are equally likely to become a fossil?
  • If you explored Floyds Fork today, what might you find that could become a fossil?
Record ideas on the board, noting misconceptions to address later.
~5 Introduce Fossilization Steps I Do: Explain key concepts Discuss the five steps of fossilization using guiding questions:
  • Death: “What might happen when this creature dies in the ocean?”
  • Burial: “How does sediment cover it?”
  • Decay: “Which parts decay first?”
  • Mineralization: “What replaces bones or shells over time?”
  • Discovery: “How do we find fossils millions of years later?”
Use diagrams and ask students to connect sediment/rock layers to Floyds Fork.
~6 Observation & Evidence Discussion We Do: Analyze fossils Show fossils from Floyds Fork. Ask:
  • Which parts are present? Which are missing?
  • Why do you think only these parts are preserved? (Preservation bias)
  • What can we infer about the creature’s size, shape, or lifestyle?
Draw observations on the board to create a shared fossil model. Prompt students to justify reasoning.
~10 Scientific Illustrator Group Modeling We Do: Predict & model environment Ask students to imagine what surrounded their fossil in the Ordovician sea and how that relates to Floyds Fork:
  • What kind of water, plants, or animals might have been nearby?
  • Where would the creature have rested or moved?
  • Draw their ideas on the board, creating a model of the creature and its habitat together.
Prompt reasoning: “Which parts are based on fossil evidence, and which are inference?” Encourage comparisons to modern Floyds Fork streams and habitats.
~15 Group Scientific Illustration You Do: Students draw Students work in small groups to illustrate their Ordovician creature and environment:
  • Use the fossil as a base.
  • Fill in missing parts using evidence and imagination.
  • Consider adaptations for movement, feeding, or survival.
  • Encourage connections to modern Floyds Fork: “How does our park today differ from the Ordovician sea?”
Circulate and ask guiding questions, prompting evidence-based reasoning.
~10 Group Presentations You Do: Share & explain Groups present their illustrations. Ask:
  • What did you infer about missing parts?
  • How did you decide what environment or other organisms to include?
  • How might these adaptations help the creature survive?
  • How does your Ordovician sea compare to Floyds Fork today?
Encourage peer discussion and constructive feedback.
~5 Reflection & Wrap-Up We Do: Summarize learning Ask students: “What surprised you about fossils? What did you have to infer?” Highlight vocabulary: fossilization, mineralization, preservation bias, adaptations, and connections to Floyds Fork.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively contribute to discussions, predictions, and drawings.
  • Evidence-Based Reasoning: Students justify inferences about missing parts and habitat using fossil evidence.
  • Collaboration: Students work together to create a coherent scientific illustration.
  • Adaptation Thinking: Higher-level students explain possible adaptations for survival.
  • Connection to Floyds Fork: Students relate fossils to local parklands and streams.
  • Presentation Skills: Students clearly describe their reconstructed creature and habitat.

Observation Tips

  • Ensure students connect fossil evidence to their inferences.
  • Check that students distinguish between observed evidence vs. imaginative reconstruction.
  • Observe group discussion, reasoning, and vocabulary usage.
  • Look for engagement with both fossils and the broader Ordovician environment.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference for educators during facilitation and observation.

Educator Cheat Sheet

  • Fossil Examples: Brachiopods, trilobites, bryozoans, crinoid stems.
  • Fossilization Reminders: Only hard parts are usually preserved; soft tissues decay. Encourage prediction and imagination.
  • Guiding Questions: Ask students to justify why certain features are likely or unlikely.
  • Ordovician Environment: Shallow seas with diverse marine life. Prompt discussion of reefs, predators, and sediment.
  • Adaptation Tip: Ask: “How might this creature move, feed, or avoid predators?”
  • Floyds Fork Connection: Always bring students back to the parklands. Compare Ordovician environments to the streams, rocks, and habitats they see today.
