Lesson Plan: Ordovician Fossils, Fossilization, & Scientific Illustration

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Educators: ____________________________

Learning Objectives

  • Use observations to determine what fossils can and cannot tell us.
  • Develop explanations for how fossils form using evidence.
  • Explain why preservation bias affects what we find as fossils.
  • Apply knowledge of Ordovician habitats to reconstruct ancient organisms.
  • Communicate scientific ideas through models and discussion.

Key Vocabulary (Introduced Through Discussion)

  • Fossil
  • Ordovician Period
  • Sediment
  • Fossilization
  • Mineralization
  • Preservation Bias
  • Limestone

Materials

  • Fossils or fossil replicas from Floyds Fork
  • Drawing paper and pencils or colored pencils
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Optional: images of scientific illustration as a model

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Guiding Questions & Educator Notes
~3 Student Arrival I Do: Set purpose Explain that students will act as scientists using clues from ancient life found at Floyds Fork.
~5 Observation First We Do: Elicit ideas Show a fossil. Ask:
  • What do you notice?
  • What might this be?
  • What makes you think that?
Record ideas without confirming answers.
~4 What’s Missing? We Do: Check understanding Ask:
  • Do you think this fossil shows the whole organism?
  • What parts might be missing?
  • Why might those parts not be here?
Listen for ideas about soft vs. hard parts.
~6 How Could This Become a Fossil? I Do: Organize thinking Guide students using questions:
  • What happens after an organism dies?
  • What would help it not disappear?
  • What parts last the longest?
Then organize student ideas into the five steps:
Die → Bury → Decay → Mineralize → Rock Forms
~4 Patterns in Fossils We Do: Sense-making Ask:
  • What kinds of organisms do we find most often as fossils?
  • What do those organisms have in common?
  • What kinds of organisms might be missing?
Introduce preservation bias after students identify the pattern.
~4 Ancient Environment We Do: Build context Ask:
  • What environment allows sediment to bury organisms?
  • What clues suggest Floyds Fork was once underwater?
Introduce the Ordovician shallow sea.
~8 Scientific Illustrator Investigation You Do: Group inquiry In groups, students examine a fossil and create a scientific illustration of the organism when alive. They decide:
  • What is based on fossil evidence?
  • What is inferred due to preservation bias?
  • How the environment influenced their choices?
Educator circulates asking probing questions.
~5 Prepare to Explain We Do: Clarify reasoning Groups prepare to explain:
  • What does the fossil show clearly?
  • What parts are missing?
  • What evidence guided your drawing?
~6 Group Presentations You Do: Share & question Groups present their organism. Audience asks:
  • What evidence supports that idea?
  • Could there be another explanation?
~4 Adaptations Extension (Optional) We Do: Deepen thinking Ask:
  • How might this organism have moved or eaten?
  • How did its features help it survive?
  • What would happen if the environment changed?
~3 Wrap-Up Reflection We Do: Synthesize Ask:
  • What can fossils tell us?
  • What can fossils not tell us?

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Students distinguish between observation and inference.
  • Students explain fossilization using reasoning.
  • Students accurately describe preservation bias.
  • Models are supported with evidence-based explanations.
  • Advanced students connect features to adaptations.

NGSS Science & Engineering Practices Alignment

  • Asking Questions: Students generate questions about fossil evidence.
  • Developing and Using Models: Scientific illustrations represent reconstructed organisms.
  • Analyzing and Interpreting Data: Fossils are treated as data with patterns.
  • Constructing Explanations: Students explain fossilization and organism features.
  • Engaging in Argument from Evidence: Students justify interpretations during presentations.
  • Communicating Information: Students share ideas through drawings and discussion.

Note: The focus is on reasoning and evidence, not a single “correct” answer.

Educator Cheat Sheet

  • Big Idea: Fossils are incomplete clues.
  • Key Prompt: “What does the fossil show—and what is missing?”
  • 5 Steps Shortcut: Die → Bury → Decay → Mineralize → Rock
  • Teaching Move: Ask first, organize thinking second.

Educator Deep Dive

  • Mineralization commonly involves calcite in limestone.
  • Ordovician Kentucky supported shallow marine ecosystems.
  • Scientific illustrations function as explanatory models.
  • Multiple interpretations are valid when supported by evidence.
