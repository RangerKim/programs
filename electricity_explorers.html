Lesson Plan: Electricity Explorers – Observing Electricity in a System

Grade Level: Upper Elementary (4th–5th Grade)
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Kentucky Science Standards Alignment

  • Observe and describe how electricity behaves in systems and interacts with objects.
  • Distinguish between conductors and insulators and how they affect energy flow.
  • Use evidence to explain static vs. current electricity and relate electricity to natural phenomena like lightning.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand and observe electricity in simple systems using hands-on stations.
  • Identify key concepts including static electricity, current electricity, conductors, insulators, attraction, repulsion, potential energy, and kinetic energy.
  • Apply observation and reasoning to explain electricity in everyday contexts and connect it to natural phenomena at Floyds Fork.

Key Vocabulary

  • Static Electricity – A buildup of electric charge on an object that doesn’t flow.
  • Current Electricity – Electric charge flowing through a circuit or path.
  • Conductor – A material that allows electricity to flow easily.
  • Insulator – A material that does not allow electricity to flow easily.
  • Attract – When objects pull toward each other due to electric charges.
  • Repel – When objects push away from each other due to electric charges.
  • Potential Energy – Stored energy that has the potential to do work.
  • Kinetic Energy – Energy of motion, energy in action.

Materials

  • 4 PVC pipes
  • 3 balloons
  • 2 beakers
  • 2 shallow containers
  • String
  • Salt and pepper
  • Paper plates
  • Empty soda can
  • Rubber ball
  • Pictures of lightning and other electricity examples from the park
  • 6 cotton or natural fiber towels
  • Dry erase markers

Guided Discovery Questions for Rangers

  • “Is this static or current electricity? How do you know?”
  • “Are the objects attracting or repelling each other? Why?”
  • “Which materials let electricity flow? Which block it?”
  • “How does potential energy turn into kinetic energy in this station?”
  • “How could electricity be seen in nature at Floyds Fork (lightning, charged surfaces)?”

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Welcome students, explain they will become Electricity Explorers investigating how electricity behaves in different systems.
~3 Introduction to Electricity I Do: Introduce electricity concepts Show pictures of lightning, explain static vs. current electricity. Connect electricity to the natural environment at Floyds Fork (lightning, friction on leaves, water movement).
~5 Review Prior Knowledge We Do: Connect prior knowledge Ask: “What is electricity? Have you seen it at home or in nature?” Discuss observations and encourage students to use vocabulary like conductor, insulator, attract, repel.
~30 Electricity Stations You Do: Hands-on exploration Station 1: Balloon Dance
- Charge up balloons with cloth, observe attraction/repulsion.
- Questions: “Do balloons attract or repel? Static or current electricity?”

Station 2: Dancing Water
- Hold charged PVC near water stream.
- Questions: “What happens to the water? Attract or repel? Static or current?”

Station 3: Salt & Pepper Separation
- Hold charged balloon over mixture.
- Questions: “Which particles move? Why? Static or current?”

Station 4: Can & Ball
- Move objects with charged PVC pipe.
- Questions: “Which moved and why? Attract or repel? Static or current?”

Station 5: Sticky Balloon
- Test what surfaces the balloon sticks to.
- Questions: “Which surfaces attract? How does this relate to conductors/insulators?”
~10 Wrap-Up & Reflection We Do: Review & reflect Discuss: “What did you learn about electricity?” “Which stations did you enjoy?”
Emphasize connection to Floyds Fork and scientific method: observation, hypothesis, testing, recording results.
Ask students to explain examples of potential vs. kinetic energy in their stations.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively engage with stations and share observations.
  • Vocabulary Use: Correctly use terms like conductor, insulator, static electricity, current electricity, attract, repel, potential and kinetic energy.
  • Evidence-Based Reasoning: Students explain why objects move or attract using vocabulary.
  • Connections to Floyds Fork: Students relate electricity to natural examples like lightning or friction phenomena in the park.

Observation Tips

  • Ensure students understand the difference between static and current electricity.
  • Check that students can identify attraction vs. repulsion and the role of materials (conductor vs insulator).
  • Observe student engagement and ability to reason scientifically through each station.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) helps rangers facilitate and observe student participation.
