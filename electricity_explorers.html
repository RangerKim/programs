Lesson Plan: Electricity Explorers

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Learning Objectives

  • Observe and experiment with static and current electricity in simple systems.
  • Use important vocabulary (insulator, conductor, attract, repel, potential vs kinetic energy) to explain electrical behavior.
  • Apply the scientific method: ask questions, make predictions, test, observe, and draw conclusions, connecting to natural phenomena in Floyds Fork.

Key Vocabulary

  • Insulator – A material that does not allow electricity to pass through easily (e.g., rubber, wood).
  • Conductor – A material that allows electricity to pass through easily (e.g., metal, water).
  • Static electricity – Electric charge that builds up on the surface of an object and can cause attraction or repulsion.
  • Current electricity – Flow of electric charge through a conductor, like in a battery or wire.
  • Attract – When two objects pull toward each other.
  • Repel – When two objects push away from each other.
  • Potential energy – Stored energy an object has due to position or charge.
  • Kinetic energy – Energy of motion.

Materials

  • 4 PVC pipes
  • 3 balloons
  • 2 beakers
  • 2 shallow containers
  • String
  • Salt & pepper mixture
  • Paper plates
  • Empty soda can
  • Rubber ball
  • Pictures of lightning and natural phenomena from Floyds Fork
  • 6 cotton/natural fiber towels
  • Dry erase markers

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Ranger 1: Welcome students, take attendance, preview electricity concepts.
Ranger 2: Begin prepping stations 1–5 (materials, setup, safety check) so they are ready for rotation.
~3 Introduction to Electricity I Do: Introduce concepts Ranger 1: Explain static vs current electricity, conductors vs insulators, attraction/repulsion. Show lightning/nature examples from Floyds Fork.
Ranger 2: Finish final station setups and test materials to ensure everything works.
~5 Vocabulary & Scientific Method Review We Do: Guided discussion Ranger 1: Review key vocabulary and introduce the scientific method steps (Ask, Hypothesize, Experiment, Observe, Conclude).
Ranger 2: Check that station instructions are clear, ready for student groups, and assist as needed.
~25 Electricity Stations You Do: Student exploration

Students rotate through 5 stations, following the scientific method. Rangers circulate and support student inquiry.

  • Station 1 – Balloon Dance
    • Question: What happens when two charged balloons are brought near each other?
    • Hypothesis: Predict attraction, repulsion, or no movement.
    • Experiment: Charge balloons with cloth, bring close/far, try near surfaces.
    • Observe: Record what happens.
    • Conclusion: Confirm if static electricity caused attraction or repulsion.
    • Rangers: Guide observations, prompt discussion on static vs current electricity.
  • Station 2 – Dancing Water
    • Question: How does a charged object affect a stream of water?
    • Hypothesis: Predict water bending toward or away.
    • Experiment: Slowly pour water into beaker, hold charged PVC near stream.
    • Observe: Record water movement and direction.
    • Conclusion: Identify static electricity in action.
    • Rangers: Encourage students to explain results using vocabulary and energy concepts.
  • Station 3 – Salt & Pepper Separation
    • Question: Can electricity separate tiny particles?
    • Hypothesis: Predict which particles will move.
    • Experiment: Mix salt & pepper, hold charged balloon nearby.
    • Observe: Note which particles move more.
    • Conclusion: Explain differences using conductors/insulators and static electricity.
    • Rangers: Prompt discussion about why some particles respond differently.
  • Station 4 – Can & Ball Movement
    • Question: Can a charged object make other objects move without touching?
    • Hypothesis: Predict can or ball movement.
    • Experiment: Charge PVC, bring near can/ball.
    • Observe: Record which moved and direction.
    • Conclusion: Identify attraction/repulsion and potential vs kinetic energy.
    • Rangers: Facilitate reasoning, help students compare results to natural electric phenomena.
  • Station 5 – Sticky Balloon Challenge
    • Question: What surfaces will a charged balloon stick to?
    • Hypothesis: Predict which surfaces will attract.
    • Experiment: Charge balloon, test on different surfaces.
    • Observe: Record which surfaces stick.
    • Conclusion: Explain using static electricity, insulators, and conductors.
    • Rangers: Ask students to relate this to energy in nature, like lightning or charged clouds.
~10 Wrap-Up Discussion We Do: Reflection & connection Ranger 1: Lead discussion on findings, favorite stations, and review scientific method.
Ranger 2: Assist students in connecting their results to natural phenomena at Floyds Fork. Reinforce vocabulary and energy concepts.
~5 Clean-Up & Questions You Do: Student reflection Both rangers: Guide clean-up, prompt reflection questions: What surprised you? What did you learn about electricity? How could scientists use experiments like these in nature?

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively engage in stations and discussion.
  • Scientific Method Understanding: Students ask questions, make hypotheses, observe, and draw conclusions.
  • Vocabulary Use: Correctly use terms like insulator, conductor, static electricity, attract, repel.
  • Observation Skills: Students notice and record effects of static electricity and energy transfer.
  • Connections to Floyds Fork: Students link experiments to real-life electricity in nature.

Observation Tips

  • Ensure students follow scientific method steps at each station.
  • Check that students can explain attraction/repulsion using vocabulary.
  • Observe curiosity, reasoning, and connection to natural phenomena in Floyds Fork.
  • Encourage students to test predictions and refine their hypotheses if needed.
  • Second ranger should be actively circulating and supporting students at stations while first ranger leads discussion.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) helps rangers track instruction and facilitation. Assigning roles ensures smooth transitions and maximum student engagement.
