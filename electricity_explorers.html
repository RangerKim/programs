Lesson Plan: Electricity Explorers

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Learning Objectives

  • Observe and experiment with static and current electricity in simple systems.
  • Use important vocabulary (insulator, conductor, attract, repel, potential vs kinetic energy) to explain electrical behavior.
  • Apply the scientific method: ask questions, make predictions, test, observe, and draw conclusions, connecting to natural phenomena in Floyds Fork.

Key Vocabulary

  • Insulator – A material that does not allow electricity to pass through easily (e.g., rubber, wood).
  • Conductor – A material that allows electricity to pass through easily (e.g., metal, water).
  • Static electricity – Electric charge that builds up on the surface of an object and can cause attraction or repulsion.
  • Current electricity – Flow of electric charge through a conductor, like in a battery or wire.
  • Attract – When two objects pull toward each other.
  • Repel – When two objects push away from each other.
  • Potential energy – Stored energy an object has due to position or charge.
  • Kinetic energy – Energy of motion.

Materials

  • 4 PVC pipes
  • 3 balloons
  • 2 beakers
  • 2 shallow containers
  • String
  • Salt & pepper mixture
  • Paper plates
  • Empty soda can
  • Rubber ball
  • Pictures of lightning and natural phenomena from Floyds Fork
  • 6 cotton/natural fiber towels
  • Dry erase markers

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Welcome students, take attendance, preview the lesson. Ranger 2 quietly sets up the first stations while Ranger 1 begins introductions. Cue students to settle and organize groups.
~3 Introduction to Electricity I Do: Explain concepts Introduce static vs current electricity, conductors vs insulators, attraction vs repulsion. Show lightning photos and discuss natural examples from Floyds Fork. Prompt: “Have you ever seen sparks or hair standing up? What do you think is happening?” Ranger 2 finishes station setup quietly.
~4 Vocabulary & Scientific Method We Do: Guided discussion Review key vocabulary. Introduce the scientific method: Ask, Hypothesize, Experiment, Observe, Conclude. Prompt: “Let’s make a hypothesis: what will happen if we bring a charged balloon near water?” Ranger 2 checks all materials and posts instructions at stations.
~2 Station Introduction I Do: Model expectations Demonstrate one station (e.g., Balloon Dance). Show how to follow the scientific method: predict, test, observe, record, conclude. Discuss safety rules. Ranger 2 ensures other stations are ready.
~25 Electricity Stations Rotations You Do: Student exploration Students rotate through the 5 stations. Rangers circulate to guide and prompt reasoning:
  • Station 1 – Balloon Dance: Charge balloons with cloth. Predict attraction/repulsion. Observe, record, and conclude. Questions: “What type of electricity is this? Why did the balloons move?”
  • Station 2 – Dancing Water: Pour water slowly into a beaker. Hold charged PVC near stream. Predict, observe, record. Questions: “Is this attraction or repulsion? Static or current?”
  • Station 3 – Salt & Pepper: Hold charged balloon near mixture. Predict which particles move. Record and conclude. Prompt: “Why do some particles respond more than others?”
  • Station 4 – Can & Ball: Use charged PVC to move can or rubber ball. Predict, observe, record. Discuss: “Which moved first? Why? Potential or kinetic energy?”
  • Station 5 – Sticky Balloon: Charge balloon, test surfaces it sticks to. Predict, observe, record. Prompt: “Which materials are conductors or insulators?”
Rangers give 3-minute warnings for transitions and prompt students to use the scientific method at each step.
~10 Group Wrap-Up Discussion We Do: Reflection & connection Ask: “Which experiment surprised you most?” “How did your observations match your predictions?” “How can we see electricity in nature, like in Floyds Fork?” Ranger 2 ensures students articulate conclusions and link concepts to natural energy examples.
~5 Clean-Up & Reflection You Do: Student reflection Students return materials, clean stations. Rangers ask: “If you were a scientist, what would you try next?” Emphasize curiosity, observation, and linking experiments to nature. Praise teamwork and scientific thinking.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively engage in stations and discussions.
  • Scientific Method Understanding: Students ask questions, make hypotheses, observe, and conclude.
  • Vocabulary Use: Correct use of insulator, conductor, static electricity, attract, repel, potential and kinetic energy.
  • Observation Skills: Students notice and record effects of static electricity and energy transfer.
  • Connections to Floyds Fork: Students link experiments to natural phenomena in the park.

Observation Tips

  • Prompt students to explain attraction/repulsion using vocabulary.
  • Check that each station clearly follows scientific method steps.
  • Observe curiosity, reasoning, and connection to nature.
  • Use 3-minute transition cues for smooth rotations.
  • Ranger 2 should actively circulate and cue students to stay on task.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference for rangers during facilitation and observation.
