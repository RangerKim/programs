Lesson Plan: Electricity Explorers

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Learning Objectives

  • Observe and explain the behavior of static and current electricity in various systems.
  • Identify conductors and insulators and describe how energy moves through a system.
  • Apply the scientific method to test hypotheses about electricity through hands-on stations.

Key Vocabulary

  • Insulator – A material that does not allow electricity to flow easily.
  • Conductor – A material that allows electricity to flow easily.
  • Static electricity – The buildup of electric charge on a surface, often resulting in attraction or repulsion.
  • Current electricity – The flow of electric charge through a circuit or conductor.
  • Attract / Repel – Forces that pull objects together or push them apart.
  • Potential vs. Kinetic Energy – Stored energy versus energy in motion.

Materials

  • 4 PVC pipes
  • 3 balloons
  • 2 beakers
  • 2 shallow containers
  • String
  • Salt and pepper mixture
  • Paper plates
  • Empty soda can
  • Rubber ball
  • Pictures of lightning and Floyds Fork park photos
  • 6 cotton or natural fiber towels
  • Dry erase markers

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Welcome students warmly, take attendance, give a brief overview of today's lesson. Set excitement and curiosity about electricity.
~5 Prior Knowledge & Questions We Do: Check understanding and prompt curiosity Ask students what they already know about electricity and energy. Encourage sharing:
  • Have you seen sparks, lightning, or objects sticking together?
  • What do you think makes things attract or repel?
  • Do you know the difference between static and current electricity?
Ask students what questions they have about electricity. Record responses on the board. Ranger 2 can start setting up stations during this discussion to save time.
~5 Vocabulary Introduction I Do: Introduce key terms Define insulator, conductor, static electricity, current electricity, attract, repel, and potential vs kinetic energy. Ask students to restate definitions in their own words. Use real examples: balloons, water, metal can, etc.
~3 Scientific Method Overview I Do: Explain steps Introduce the scientific method: Question, Hypothesis, Materials, Procedure, Observation, Conclusion. Explain that students will apply this method at each station.
~25 Electricity Stations (1–5) You Do: Hands-on exploration Students rotate through stations applying the scientific method. Rangers circulate to guide observations, questions, and reasoning.
  • Station 1 – Balloon Dance: Charge balloons with cloth. Predict: what will happen when balloons are brought close together? Observe attraction or repulsion. Identify static or current electricity. Record findings.
  • Station 2 – Dancing Water: Slowly pour water. Charge PVC pipe and hold near stream. Predict how water moves. Observe, record, and identify electricity type. Discuss observations with a partner.
  • Station 3 – Salt & Pepper: Hold charged balloon near mixture. Predict which particles will move first. Observe separation. Determine which is attracted most and discuss why.
  • Station 4 – Can & Ball: Use charged PVC pipe to move can and ball without touching. Predict which moves and why. Identify forces: attract or repel. Determine static or current electricity.
  • Station 5 – Sticky Balloon: Charge balloon and test surfaces around the room. Predict which objects will stick. Observe and record outcomes. Discuss observations as a group.
Ranger 2 ensures all materials are ready at each station and helps students safely and effectively rotate through them.
~5 Wrap-Up & Reflection We Do: Review findings Discuss what students learned at each station. Ask guiding questions:
  • Which stations showed static electricity versus current electricity?
  • How did the material type affect movement of objects?
  • How can we see these concepts in nature or in the park? (e.g., lightning, charged objects)
  • What surprised you? What did you enjoy the most?
Rangers help students make connections to real-world observations and reinforce vocabulary.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively engage in station activities and discussions.
  • Scientific Method Use: Students make predictions, observe carefully, and draw conclusions.
  • Vocabulary Understanding: Correctly define and use electricity-related terms.
  • Connections: Students relate electricity to real-life examples, including the parklands.
  • Questioning & Thinking: Students ask questions and reason through observations at each station.

Observation Tips

  • Check that each activity clearly shows I Do / We Do / You Do roles.
  • Look for students connecting concepts to natural phenomena.
  • Observe engagement, reasoning, and ability to follow the scientific method.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference for rangers during facilitation. Labels in text clearly indicate the type of activity.
