Lesson Plan: Electricity Explorers

Grade Level: Upper Elementary
Duration: ~60 minutes
Location: Classroom / Floyds Fork connection
Instructors: Rangers (Erin & Lori)

Learning Objectives

  • Observe and experiment with static and current electricity in simple systems.
  • Use important vocabulary (insulator, conductor, attract, repel, potential vs kinetic energy) to explain electrical behavior.
  • Apply the scientific method: ask questions, make predictions, test, observe, and draw conclusions, connecting to natural phenomena in Floyds Fork.

Key Vocabulary

  • Insulator – A material that does not allow electricity to pass through easily (e.g., rubber, wood).
  • Conductor – A material that allows electricity to pass through easily (e.g., metal, water).
  • Static electricity – Electric charge that builds up on the surface of an object and can cause attraction or repulsion.
  • Current electricity – Flow of electric charge through a conductor, like in a battery or wire.
  • Attract – When two objects pull toward each other.
  • Repel – When two objects push away from each other.
  • Potential energy – Stored energy an object has due to position or charge.
  • Kinetic energy – Energy of motion.

Materials

  • 4 PVC pipes
  • 3 balloons
  • 2 beakers
  • 2 shallow containers
  • String
  • Salt & pepper mixture
  • Paper plates
  • Empty soda can
  • Rubber ball
  • Pictures of lightning and natural phenomena from Floyds Fork
  • 6 cotton/natural fiber towels
  • Dry erase markers

Lesson Procedure (Approx. Minutes)

Time Activity Instructional Role Details / Ranger Notes
~2 Student Arrival I Do: Greet and set expectations Welcome students warmly, take attendance, briefly preview the lesson. Make students excited about exploring electricity.
Ranger 2 sets up stations while Ranger 1 introduces concepts.
Cue students to put backpacks aside and assign station groups early.
~3 Introduction to Electricity I Do: Introduce concepts Explain static vs current electricity, conductors vs insulators, attraction and repulsion. Show photos of lightning and natural examples from Floyds Fork.
Ranger 2 continues station setup and checks that all materials are ready.
Ask students: “Have you ever noticed your hair sticking up after sliding down a slide?” “What do you think causes lightning?”
~5 Vocabulary & Scientific Method Review We Do: Guided discussion Review key vocabulary and introduce the scientific method: Ask, Hypothesize, Experiment, Observe, Conclude.
Ranger 2 ensures stations have instructions posted and materials organized.
Prompt: “Let’s make a hypothesis about what might happen if we bring a balloon near water.”
~25 Electricity Stations You Do: Student exploration Students rotate through 5 stations, applying the scientific method at each:
  • Station 1 – Balloon Dance: Charge balloons with cloth. Predict what will happen when balloons come close together. Observe attraction/repulsion. Record results. Discuss static vs current electricity.
  • Station 2 – Dancing Water: Pour water slowly into a beaker. Bring charged PVC near water without touching. Predict what will happen, observe, and record results. Discuss attraction/repulsion.
  • Station 3 – Salt & Pepper: Hold charged balloon near salt & pepper mixture. Predict which particles will move. Observe and record results. Discuss why some particles respond more strongly.
  • Station 4 – Can & Ball: Try to move a soda can and rubber ball with charged PVC pipe. Predict which moves first. Observe and record. Discuss potential vs kinetic energy.
  • Station 5 – Sticky Balloon Challenge: Test which surfaces the charged balloon sticks to. Predict and record results. Discuss insulators vs conductors and static electricity.
Rangers circulate to prompt thinking: “Why did this happen?” “Which type of electricity is this?” “Can you connect this to nature?” Signal 3-minute warnings for smooth transitions.
~10 Wrap-Up Discussion We Do: Reflection & connection Review observations and favorite stations. Discuss how students applied the scientific method.
Ask: “Which experiment surprised you?” “How is this similar to what happens in nature at Floyds Fork?”
Ranger 2 supports students in connecting observations to real-world examples of energy in nature.
~5 Clean-Up & Questions You Do: Student reflection Guide clean-up of stations. Ask: “If you were a scientist, what experiment would you try next?” Praise curiosity and scientific reasoning. Reset stations for the next group.

Assessment / Look-Fors

  • Participation: Students actively engage in stations and discussions.
  • Scientific Method Understanding: Students ask questions, make hypotheses, observe, and draw conclusions.
  • Vocabulary Use: Correctly use terms like insulator, conductor, static electricity, attract, repel, potential and kinetic energy.
  • Observation Skills: Students notice and record effects of static electricity and energy transfer.
  • Connections to Floyds Fork: Students link experiments to natural phenomena in the park.

Observation Tips

  • Check that each activity clearly follows the scientific method steps.
  • Prompt students to explain attraction/repulsion using vocabulary.
  • Observe curiosity, reasoning, and connection to nature.
  • Use 3-minute transition cues for smooth station rotations.
  • Ranger 2 should actively circulate, assist, and cue students to stay on task.

Note: Color coding I Do (Green), We Do (Yellow), You Do (Blue) is for quick visual reference for rangers during facilitation and observation.
